# Outcome Comparison Lens v0.1

Status: candidate comparison lens. Not validation. Not proof. Not active TRACE spine. Not a scoring system. Not Kernel v0.3.

Purpose: preserve a non-numeric way to compare lived outcomes across affected entities without pretending exact measurement is possible.

This came from the 3000-year game / entity-wellbeing discussion. It must remain a lens, not a final metric.

---

## 1. Problem

Systems often concentrate benefit in a few entities while distributing burden, fear, coercion, scarcity, or suffering across many others.

A simple example:

- a king or elite actor receives high benefit;
- the population below experiences low agency, high fear, extraction, or persistent suffering.

A method may need to compare such systems without claiming exact utility calculus.

---

## 2. Constraint

You cannot precisely measure:

- happiness,
- suffering,
- consciousness,
- sentience,
- agency,
- comparative life-value across species or minds.

Do not pretend otherwise.

```trace
measurement_limit :=
  no_exact_common_unit_for_lived_experience
```

This lens exists because decisions still require comparison under uncertainty.

---

## 3. Do Not Make a Single Number

Do not calculate a total score.

Do not assign fake precision.

Do not hide philosophical assumptions inside arithmetic.

```trace
forbidden :=
  total_wellbeing_number
  + fake_sentience_weights
  + precision_theatre
```

This file is a structured comparison aid, not a utilitarian calculator.

---

## 4. Five Directional Factors

Compare systems across five factors.

### 1. Scope

How many entities are affected?

- narrow impact,
- group impact,
- population impact,
- cross-generational impact,
- multispecies / ecological impact.

### 2. Intensity

How strong is the experience?

- mild discomfort,
- severe suffering,
- terror,
- deprivation,
- meaningful flourishing,
- safety,
- dignity,
- joy.

### 3. Duration

How long does the condition last?

- momentary,
- episodic,
- chronic,
- life-shaping,
- intergenerational.

### 4. Agency

Can affected entities change, exit, contest, adapt, or influence the condition?

- trapped,
- partially constrained,
- able to contest,
- able to exit,
- able to shape future conditions.

### 5. Reversibility

Can harm be repaired or corrected?

- reversible,
- partially repairable,
- scarred but survivable,
- irreversible,
- compounding.

---

## 5. Compression

```trace
outcome_comparison :=
  scope
  + intensity
  + duration
  + agency
  + reversibility
```

This is not a sum.

It is a structured view.

---

## 6. Use Pattern

Compare System A and System B side by side.

Ask:

- Which system affects more entities?
- Which creates more intense suffering or flourishing?
- Which conditions persist longer?
- Which preserves more agency?
- Which preserves more reversibility?

A system is structurally worse when it produces broad, intense, durable, low-agency, irreversible harm.

A system is structurally safer when it preserves agency and reversibility across affected subjects.

---

## 7. Example Shape

System A:

- high peak benefit for few;
- widespread burden for many;
- low agency among affected subjects;
- harm difficult to reverse.

System B:

- lower peak benefit;
- more distributed basic wellbeing;
- higher agency;
- stronger correction routes;
- more reversible failures.

Outcome lens read:

```trace
System_B_likely_safer_than_System_A
because:
  agency_higher
  + reversibility_higher
  + distributed_harm_lower
```

This is a directional read, not proof.

---

## 8. Sentience / Entity Weighting Boundary

Different entities may have different capacities for suffering, memory, agency, anticipation, relationship, and future-space.

This matters.

But do not pretend the weighting is settled.

```trace
sentience_weighting := unsettled_boundary
```

If entity weighting is required, mark it explicitly as an assumption.

Example assumption statement:

"This comparison treats human self-reflective agency as carrying additional weight, while still preserving nonhuman suffering as morally relevant."

Do not bury that assumption.

---

## 9. Relationship to TRACE

This lens supports TRACE by making affected-subject consequence visible.

It especially supports:

- repair analysis,
- agency-window analysis,
- future-space analysis,
- irreversibility analysis,
- public/system comparison.

It does not replace the current TRACE spine.

```trace
Outcome_Lens_role :=
  support_lens
  not:
    active_operator
    OR scoring_engine
    OR ethics_theory
```

---

## 10. Failure Modes

Demote or freeze this lens if it becomes:

- a single number,
- a fake utilitarian calculator,
- a way to erase individual rights,
- a way to justify sacrificing vulnerable subjects,
- a way to smuggle human preferences as math,
- a way to flatten nonhuman or disabled experience,
- an aesthetic ranking game,
- a substitute for evidence.

```trace
demote_if :=
  number_theatre
  OR sacrifice_laundering
  OR sentience_weight_hidden
  OR distribution_erased
  OR evidence_replaced_by_score
```

---

## 11. Comparator Fields

Before promotion or strong use, compare with:

- utilitarian ethics,
- capability approach,
- welfare economics,
- animal welfare science,
- disability ethics,
- population ethics,
- human rights / anti-sacrifice constraints,
- quality-adjusted life-year debates and critiques,
- restorative justice and repair theory.

If these fields already carry the same work with less distortion, demote this to translation note.

---

## 12. Current Status

Candidate support lens.

Safe current use:

- compare systems directionally,
- expose hidden distributed burden,
- identify agency/reversibility collapse,
- support case discussion.

Unsafe current use:

- rank lives numerically,
- certify policy,
- justify coercion,
- replace domain expertise,
- claim solved moral calculus.

---

## 13. Final Line

Compare directions under uncertainty.

Prefer structures that preserve agency and reversibility across affected entities.

Never let the lens become a number that hides the lives beneath it.

End.
